SegLLM: Multi-round Reasoning Segmentation

Making image segmentation conversational by feeding previous mask outputs back into the LLM to enable coherent, multi-turn interaction.

AM
QB
Aran Moradi & Qianyu Bu
~10 min read
00 Motivation

Interactive image segmentation plays an important role in medical imaging, helping doctors precisely annotate structures like tumours, organs, and blood vessels to support diagnosis and surgical planning [1]. In practice, doctors rarely ask just one question and stop. Instead, they work through a series of steps: first locating a tumour, then checking the surrounding vasculature, then isolating a specific organ. This process is naturally multi-round. Research has shown that interactive segmentation tools can reduce annotation workload by three to four times compared to traditional manual annotation [2]. The problem is that existing tools treat each query independently. Every time a user asks a new question, they have to re-establish the full context from scratch. This is especially frustrating in medical settings, where anatomical structures are closely related and the result of one segmentation step is almost always the starting point for the next. Bridging this gap, giving models the ability to remember previous interactions, is exactly what motivates the work we discuss in this post.

01 Background & Problem
1.1 What is Image Segmentation?

Image segmentation is the task of partitioning a digital image into multiple regions by assigning a label to every pixel, such that pixels with the same label share certain visual characteristics [3]. It is one of the most fundamental problems in computer vision, underpinning applications from medical imaging and autonomous driving to scene understanding and interactive editing. Put simply: instead of just saying "there is a dog somewhere in this photo" (classification), or drawing a box around it (detection), segmentation traces the dog's exact outline, pixel by pixel.

Not all segmentation tasks ask the same question, though. It helps to separate three flavours of the problem, since later sections build directly on this distinction:

Semantic Segmentation
Labels every pixel with a category, such as "road", "sky", or "person", but does not distinguish between individual instances. Two different people in the photo are both just labelled "person"; the model does not separate person A from person B.
Instance Segmentation
Goes a step further: it separates individual objects of the same category from one another. Person A gets one mask, person B gets a different mask, even though both are labelled "person".
Panoptic Segmentation
Combines both: countable objects (people, cars) are separated into instances, while uncountable background regions (sky, grass, road) are labelled semantically. Every pixel in the image ends up covered.
1.2 Before Deep Learning: Rule-based & Statistical Methods

Before deep learning, segmentation relied on hand-designed rules and simple statistics over pixel values, rather than features learned from data. Three families of techniques dominated this era:

01
Thresholding. The simplest approach: pick a brightness or colour value as a cutoff, and group pixels into "above" and "below". One of the most widely used variants automatically picks this cutoff by finding the value that best separates the image's two dominant brightness modes [4]. Works well when the object of interest has a clearly different intensity from the background, e.g. separating dark text from a white page.
02
Edge detection. Looks for sharp, local changes in pixel intensity, on the assumption that object boundaries usually correspond to such changes. Classic filters such as the Canny edge detector [5] scan the image for these jumps and connect them into outlines.
03
Clustering. Statistical methods such as k-means or the watershed transform [6] group pixels by similarity in colour, intensity, or texture, without needing an explicit boundary rule. Pixels that "look alike" end up in the same region.

These methods are fast, require no training data, and are easy to reason about. However, they all rely on hand-crafted assumptions about what makes pixels belong together, such as a fixed brightness cutoff, a fixed notion of "sharp change", or a fixed similarity measure. Real-world images often break these assumptions: shadows and uneven lighting confuse thresholding, textured backgrounds create false edges, and objects that share a similar colour to their surroundings get grouped together incorrectly by clustering. None of these methods have any real concept of an "object"; they only look at pixel statistics, not semantics.

What is the Canny Operator?
The Canny edge detector first smooths the image to reduce noise, then computes the intensity gradient at every pixel to find where brightness changes fastest. It keeps only the pixels that are local maxima along the gradient direction (thinning wide blurry edges down to a single crisp line), and finally uses two thresholds to decide which edges are strong enough to keep, linking weaker edges to nearby strong ones so faint but connected boundaries aren't discarded outright.
Canny, TPAMI 1986 [5]
What is the Structure Tensor?
For each pixel, we look at a small neighbourhood and compute the image gradient at every point inside it. Instead of just measuring gradient strength like Canny does, the structure tensor collects these gradients into a small 2×2 matrix. This matrix has the same form as a covariance matrix, except it is built from gradient vectors instead of data samples. Its eigenvalues tell us what kind of region we are looking at: if both are small, the area is flat; if one is large and one is small, we are looking at an edge; and if both are large, we are looking at a corner. This is the mechanism behind the Harris corner detector, and it captures more information than a simple, single-pixel gradient check.
Harris & Stephens, Alvey 1988 [7]

1.3 Deep Learning-based Image Segmentation

Deep learning changed this by letting the model learn what a "boundary" or "region" looks like directly from data, rather than relying on hand-crafted rules. The field's evolution can be seen as a series of steps, each solving a limitation of the one before it:

FCN
Fully Convolutional Networks [8] took standard image-classification networks (which end in a single "this image is a cat" prediction) and replaced their final layers so that every pixel gets its own prediction instead. This made pixel-wise, end-to-end labelling possible for the first time and is the basis of modern semantic segmentation. Its main limitation: it labels categories, but has no notion of separate object instances.
Mask R-CNN
Mask R-CNN [9] addressed exactly that gap. It first detects individual objects with bounding boxes, like a detector would, and then predicts a pixel mask inside each box. This is what gives it the ability to separate person A from person B, achieving true instance segmentation. The trade-off is that it depends on a two-stage pipeline (detecting boxes, then segmenting inside them), which adds complexity and can allow detection mistakes to carry over into the mask.
MaskFormer
MaskFormer [10] simplified this by borrowing the transformer's "query" mechanism: instead of first drawing boxes, the model learns a fixed set of queries that each directly predict one mask and its category. This unifies semantic, instance, and panoptic segmentation under one framework, removing the need for separate specialised pipelines.
SAM
SAM (Segment Anything Model) [11] marked a further shift towards generality. Trained on over 1 billion masks, SAM can segment almost any object in almost any image without needing to be retrained, simply by giving it a point, a box, or a rough scribble as a prompt. This made segmentation far more flexible, but the prompt itself still has to be a simple visual cue. SAM has no way to understand a prompt like "the person talking on the phone", since that requires reading and reasoning about language rather than just clicking a point.

By the time we reach SAM, most modern deep-learning segmentation models, especially the ones that accept a text query rather than just a click or a box, converge on a shared, three-part architecture. It is worth sketching this out once, since SegLLM (discussed in Section 2) is itself a variation on exactly this pattern:

Figure 0: The typical modern segmentation architecture. Separate encoders process the image and text, and their outputs are fused inside a mask decoder that predicts the final mask.

Where this leaves us: every method above needs an explicit, low-level prompt, such as a category name, a box, or a click. None of them can take a free-form sentence and work out on their own which pixels the sentence is describing. That gap is exactly where reasoning segmentation, discussed next, comes in.
1.4 Reasoning Segmentation

Reasoning in Large Language Models refers to the ability to solve complex problems through multi-step inference, drawing on learned knowledge to chain intermediate conclusions towards a final answer [12]. Unlike simple pattern matching, LLM reasoning involves understanding context, resolving ambiguity, and following implicit logical structure. This capability has been demonstrated across diverse tasks including mathematical problem solving, commonsense inference, and question answering [13].

It helps to make this concrete before moving on, since the distinction between "reasoning segmentation" and ordinary segmentation is easy to lose sight of once the architecture gets more complex. The key difference is whether the query names the target object directly, or only describes it indirectly.

Label-based Query
"Segment the orange." The query names the target object directly. The model only has to match the word "orange" to the correct region of the image; no extra knowledge or inference is required.
Reasoning Query
"Segment the food that is high in Vitamin C." The target object is never named. The model first has to recall, using its general world knowledge, which foods are rich in Vitamin C, and only then can it work out which object in the image that description points to.
Reasoning Query (Relational)
"Segment the bag held by the person wearing the red jacket." Here the model has to resolve two objects and the relationship between them: first find the person wearing the red jacket, then find the bag that person is holding. There is no single label for the target at all; it only exists in terms of its relationship to another object.

This is exactly the gap identified in Section 1.3: a model like SAM can segment "the orange" perfectly well once it is given that label, but it has no way of resolving "the food that is high in Vitamin C" into a label on its own, and it has no mechanism at all for reasoning about relationships between objects, as the third example requires. That resolving step, working out which object a description refers to before any segmentation happens, is what reasoning segmentation adds.

The two reasoning queries above are instances of a task called reasoning segmentation, in contrast to the label-based query. More formally: given an implicit, complex natural language query, the model must reason about image content to understand relationships, context, and intent before producing a segmentation mask [14]. As the examples show, this goes well beyond simply matching a text label to a visual region. Models such as LLaVA [15] showed that aligning visual encoders with LLMs through instruction tuning enables rich cross-modal reasoning. Building on this, LISA [14] operationalised reasoning segmentation by introducing a learned [SEG] token as a clean interface between language-level reasoning and pixel-level mask output, without any handcrafted heuristics for when or what to segment.

What is the [SEG] Token?
[SEG] is a single new token added to the LLM's vocabulary. Its job is not to carry meaning on its own; it simply signals to the rest of the system that a segmentation mask needs to be produced here, telling the downstream mask decoder when to act. What matters more than the token itself is its hidden state: the vector the LLM computes internally at the position of that token, taken from the model's last hidden layer. Because the LLM has just reasoned through the entire query and image together, this vector ends up containing exactly the semantic information the mask decoder needs, including what the task is, which object is being referred to, and how that object relates to others in the scene. Since this hidden state is what actually gets passed forward and compared against the image features, it is often referred to as the query vector.
Lai et al., CVPR 2024 [14]

This changes the three-part architecture from Section 1.3 by inserting a reasoning step in the middle. The encoders no longer feed the mask decoder directly. Instead, their outputs go into an LLM that reasons over both, and only the LLM's output (the [SEG] token's hidden state) is passed on to the mask decoder:

Figure 0b: Reasoning segmentation architecture. The LLM sits between the encoders and the mask decoder, reasoning over both modalities before emitting the [SEG] token that drives mask prediction.

Note: in many modern approaches, the "LLM" here is actually a multimodal VLM (Vision-Language Model), which already has the components needed to handle both image and text internally. In that case, a separate text encoder is not needed, since the user's text query is fed straight into the model, which tokenizes and processes it itself alongside the visual input. This is exactly the case for LLaVA-based systems like LISA and SegLLM, discussed next.
1.5 The Problem: Single-turn Limitation

Despite these advances, current LLM-based segmentation models are limited to single-turn queries. Once a mask is generated, the model has no memory of it, and users cannot follow up with queries that reference previous segmentation outputs.

Looking back at the architecture in Figure 0b makes this limitation concrete. Information only flows in one direction: the image and text go into the LLM, the LLM emits a [SEG] token, and the mask decoder turns that into a mask. Once the mask decoder has produced its output, the pipeline is finished. There is no path for that mask, or any other trace of what just happened, to flow back into the LLM. This means that if a user asks a follow-up question that depends on the previous result, the LLM has no way to access what it just segmented, and it has no record that a previous round of segmentation even took place. To support multi-round reasoning, or to let the model reason about an object based on where a previously segmented object was, this feedback path would have to exist. In the architecture as it stands, it simply does not.

This limitation breaks the natural flow of interactive segmentation, which is increasingly important in real-world annotation, editing, and scene understanding pipelines.

Round 1"Highlight the man with the white hoodie."
Round 2"Mask the bike behind the man from the previous prompt."

The second query is beyond what most existing models can handle, since it depends entirely on the result of the first.

This might seem like an easy gap to close. When we use a chatbot such as ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini in a browser, we can have a long, multi-turn conversation, and the model clearly remembers what was said earlier in the same chat. Since reasoning segmentation is built on exactly this kind of LLM, it seems reasonable to expect that it should be able to do the same.

02 Method

SegLLM addresses this by introducing a feedback loop: previous segmentation outputs from the mask decoder are fed back into the LLM, while conversation context is injected into the mask decoder's query input. This bidirectional coupling is what prior work lacked.

2.1 Model Structure

The image encoder (CLIP or DINOv2) transforms the input image into patch representations and converts them into embeddings. A projection layer then aligns these visual embeddings with the LLM's text embedding space, allowing joint processing of both modalities. The LLM (LLaVA) processes the combined input and generates an output sequence, emitting a special token [SEG] whenever segmentation output is required, serving as a learned interface to the mask decoder.

What is CLIP?
CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining) is a model developed by OpenAI that learns to connect images and text by training on hundreds of millions of image-caption pairs. Instead of learning to classify images into fixed categories, CLIP learns a shared embedding space where similar images and texts are placed close together. This makes it very good at understanding visual content through natural language, even for concepts it has never seen labelled before.
Radford et al., ICML 2021 [16]
What is LLaVA?
LLaVA (Large Language and Vision Assistant) is a multimodal model that connects a visual encoder with a large language model through a simple projection layer. It is fine-tuned on visual instruction data, teaching the model to answer questions and follow instructions about images. LLaVA serves as the backbone LLM in SegLLM, providing the language reasoning capability that drives the multi-round conversation.
Liu et al., NeurIPS 2023 [15]

Figure 2: SegLLM architecture overview. Previous mask outputs are fed back into the LLM via Mask Encoder and Box Encoder, enabling multi-round reasoning segmentation.

2.2 Mask Encoding Module

For each mask generated by the decoder, the Mask Encoding Module produces two complementary embeddings. The mask embedding captures semantic information: pixels outside the mask are set to black, the image is cropped according to the bounding box, and the result is passed through the CLIP-ViT encoder. The raw embeddings are then projected through an MLP layer to match the input dimension of the LLM. The bounding box embedding encodes the spatial location of the mask within the original image. Both embeddings are fed sequentially back into the LLM for each mask, giving the model a full record of what was previously segmented and where.

Key insight: the [SEG] token bridges language understanding and pixel-level segmentation without handcrafted heuristics. When to segment is learned, not programmed.
2.3 Mask-Aware Decoding Module

The Mask-Aware Decoding Module uses two special tokens to handle reference and target separately. The [REF] token represents an object that has already been segmented, allowing the model to track what a user might refer to in subsequent rounds. The [SEG] token carries information about the current segmentation target.

These tokens are combined into two queries. In the first query [REF][PAD], the model learns the identity of the reference object, grounding the token to a specific previously segmented region. In the second query [REF][SEG], the model jointly attends to both the reference and the current task, enabling it to resolve cross-round references and produce the correct target segmentation mask.

03 Experiment
3.1 Dataset

The training data consists of multi-round conversational segmentation queries built on top of several existing public datasets, including RefCOCO, Visual Genome, PACO-LVIS, and COCO-Stuff. The dataset covers a diverse set of object relationships, including positional, hierarchical, and interactional relationships, with conversation templates refined using GPT-4 to ensure natural language fluency.

Figure 3: Dataset overview showing multi-round conversational segmentation examples across different object relationship types.

3.2 Inference Pipeline

At inference time, SegLLM processes each conversational round sequentially. The following steps describe how a single round is executed, with previous mask outputs carried forward as context into each new round.

01
The user provides an image and a natural language query. The image is passed through the image encoder to produce visual embeddings, which are aligned to the LLM input space via the projection layer.
02
If previous rounds exist, the mask embeddings and bounding box embeddings from earlier segmentations are prepended to the input stream, making the LLM aware of all previously identified objects.
03
The LLM processes the combined visual, textual, and mask context. When the model determines that segmentation is required, it emits a [SEG] token in its output sequence.
04
The mask decoder receives the [REF] and [SEG] token embeddings from the LLM. It first runs the [REF][PAD] query to ground the reference object, then the [REF][SEG] query to produce the target segmentation mask.
05
The newly generated mask is encoded by the Mask Encoding Module into a mask embedding and a bounding box embedding. These are stored and fed back into the LLM in the next round, closing the feedback loop.
3.3 Training

SegLLM is trained end-to-end on the dataset described in Section 3.1, with the image encoder and mask encoder kept frozen, while the LLM, projection layer, and mask-aware decoding module are fine-tuned.

The training follows a two-objective setup. A language modelling loss supervises the text generation output of the LLM, ensuring coherent multi-round responses. A segmentation loss supervises the mask decoder output, optimising pixel-level accuracy. Both losses are applied jointly, so the model learns to balance conversational fluency with segmentation quality across all rounds simultaneously.

4.1 Evaluation Metrics

SegLLM is evaluated across three types of tasks, each with its own primary metric. The primary baseline for all comparisons is LISA, the previous state-of-the-art single-round reasoning segmentation model.

Task Metric Description
Referring Expression Segmentation (RES) cIoU Cumulative Intersection over Union, measuring the ratio of intersection to union of predicted and ground truth masks accumulated across all test samples.
Referring Expression Comprehension (REC) Acc@0.5 Percentage of predictions where the predicted bounding box has an IoU of at least 0.5 with the ground truth box.
Multi-round Segmentation (MRSeg) cIoU per round cIoU reported per conversational round, evaluating how well the model maintains segmentation accuracy as the number of rounds and relational complexity increases.

The MRSeg benchmark covers four query types of increasing complexity: referring queries, hierarchical queries, interactional queries, and relational queries. As the rounds progress, the complexity of interaction and memory retention increases, leading to a natural decline in cIoU for all models. SegLLM consistently outperforms LISA across all rounds, with the performance gap widening in later rounds where cross-round reasoning matters most.

4.2 Ablation Study

The ablation study isolates the contribution of each of SegLLM's two core modules. The results confirm that both components are necessary for strong multi-round performance.

Configuration Impact Finding
Full SegLLM Baseline Best performance across all multi-round and single-round benchmarks.
Without Mask Encoding Module Large drop The LLM no longer receives any information about previously generated masks, effectively reducing the model to a single-round system. Multi-round performance degrades significantly.
Without [REF][PAD] query Moderate drop The decoder loses its ability to ground the reference object. The model can still generate masks but struggles with queries that depend on spatial or hierarchical relationships to prior outputs.
Diverse query templates (RefCOCO) +9.6% When tested with rephrased query variants rather than fixed expressions, SegLLM outperforms LISA by 9.6%, suggesting that multi-round training on varied conversational data improves generalisation to different query phrasings.
4.3 Conclusions

SegLLM does a great job at bringing image segmentation into a multi-round conversation setting, beating existing methods by a large margin while also achieving better results on single-round tasks. The feedback architecture generalises well across both settings.

05 Our Opinion

We find SegLLM to be a genuinely interesting piece of work. It solves a real and previously underexplored limitation: the inability to follow up on previously segmented objects, which significantly raises the ceiling for interactive segmentation.

Four open concerns
Error propagation. If the model makes a wrong segmentation in round one, that error may carry into all subsequent rounds, since each round builds directly on top of previous outputs. This is particularly concerning because the Mask Encoding Module encodes the incorrect mask's spatial bounding box directly into the LLM's input stream, meaning corrupted location information gets passed forward as if it were reliable context. The MRSeg results already hint at this: cIoU declines as rounds increase, which may partly reflect accumulated error rather than just increased task complexity. It would be valuable to know how the model performs when intentionally given a wrong first-round mask.
Order sensitivity. The model appears sensitive to conversation ordering, since the [REF] token mechanism is built up sequentially with no global attention over all historical rounds. For example, asking "segment the person" first and then "segment the bag she is holding" may give a different result than asking about the bag before establishing the person as a reference. Drawing on NLP evaluation practice, where reversed options or paraphrased queries are standard robustness tests, a similar approach should be applied here. Testing SegLLM with reordered conversation histories would give a clearer picture of whether the model truly understands relationships or is simply pattern-matching the order of prompts.
Scalability over long conversations. As the number of rounds increases, the mask embeddings and bounding box embeddings fed back into the LLM grow linearly. The paper only evaluates up to 8 rounds on the MRSeg benchmark. In longer real-world conversations — such as a radiologist annotating dozens of structures in a single session — the context window may become a limiting factor. It is unclear how the model degrades when the number of historical mask embeddings exceeds what the LLM can effectively attend to.
No correction mechanism. If a user realises that a mask from an earlier round was wrong, there is no way to correct it within the framework. The incorrect mask has already been encoded into the conversation history and will continue to influence all future rounds. A practical deployment of SegLLM would need some form of undo or correction interface, but the paper does not address this. This is especially relevant in medical settings, where annotation errors can have serious downstream consequences.
