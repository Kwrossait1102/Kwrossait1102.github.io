SegLLM: Multi-round Reasoning Segmentation

Making image segmentation conversational by feeding previous mask outputs back into the LLM to enable coherent, multi-turn interaction.

AM
QB
Aran Moradi & Qianyu Bu
~25 min read
00 Motivation

Interactive image segmentation lets a user refine what a model segments through natural language, rather than committing to a single fixed query up front. It matters wherever segmentation is used in an exploratory, step-by-step way, such as image editing, robotics, video annotation, and medical imaging [1].

In a clinical scenario, a doctor rarely asks just one question and stops. Instead, they work through a series of steps: first locating a tumour, then checking the surrounding vasculature, then isolating a specific organ. This process is naturally multi-round, where the result of one segmentation step is almost always the starting point for the next.

The problem is that existing tools treat each query independently. Every time a user asks a new question, they have to re-establish the full context from scratch. This is especially frustrating in medical settings, where anatomical structures are closely related. Bridging this gap, giving models memory of previous rounds, is what this post is about.

The rest of this post is organised as follows. Section 1 sets up what image segmentation is and why current LLM-based models cannot handle follow-up queries. Section 2 walks through SegLLM's architecture and its two core changes: the mask encoding module and the mask-aware decoder. Section 3 covers the training data and setup, Section 4 presents the results, and Section 5 shares our own critique of the paper.

01 Background & Problem
1.1 Image Segmentation

Image segmentation assigns a label to every pixel in an image, tracing objects out at pixel resolution rather than just detecting or classifying them [3]. Instead of saying "there is a dog somewhere in this photo", or drawing a box around it, segmentation outlines the dog exactly, pixel by pixel. It is one of the most fundamental problems in computer vision, underpinning applications from medical imaging and autonomous driving to interactive editing.

1.2 The Modern Approach

Modern segmentation models are almost all built on deep learning, and the ones that accept a text query rather than just a click or a box converge on a shared three-part architecture:

Figure 1: The typical modern segmentation architecture. The image and the text query are processed by separate encoders, and their outputs are fused inside a mask decoder that predicts the final mask.

MaskFormer [4] was the first to fit the different kinds of segmentation tasks into this three-part recipe, by borrowing the transformer's "query" mechanism: a fixed set of learned queries each predicts one mask and its category directly.

SAM (Segment Anything Model) [5] then pushed the same recipe to arbitrary objects: trained on over one billion masks, it can segment almost any object in almost any image given a point, box, or scribble as a prompt, without retraining.

What's missing: every method above needs an explicit, low-level prompt: a category name, a bounding box, or a click. None of them can take a free-form sentence like "the food high in Vitamin C" and work out on their own which pixels the sentence is describing. Reasoning segmentation closes that gap.
1.3 Reasoning Segmentation

Large Language Models can do more than pattern-match text. They can chain intermediate steps to solve problems that require background knowledge and inference [6]. For example, they can work out the answer to a word problem, or resolve what "she" refers to in a paragraph [7]. This ability to reason is what makes it possible to plug an LLM into segmentation in the first place.

The key difference between reasoning segmentation and ordinary segmentation is whether the query names the target object directly, or only describes it indirectly:

Label-based Query
"Segment the orange." The query names the target object directly. The model only has to match the word "orange" to the correct region of the image; no extra knowledge or inference is required.
Reasoning Query
"Segment the food that is high in Vitamin C." The target object is never named. The model first has to recall, using its general world knowledge, which foods are rich in Vitamin C, and only then can it work out which object in the image that description points to.
Reasoning Query (Relational)
"Segment the bag held by the person wearing the red jacket." Here the model has to resolve two objects and the relationship between them: first find the person wearing the red jacket, then find the bag that person is holding. There is no single label for the target at all; it only exists in terms of its relationship to another object.

A model like SAM can segment "the orange" perfectly well once it is given that label, but it has no way of resolving "the food that is high in Vitamin C" into a label on its own, and no mechanism at all for reasoning about relationships between objects, as the third example requires. That resolving step, working out which object a description refers to before any segmentation happens, is what reasoning segmentation adds. More formally: given an implicit natural language query, the model must reason about image content to understand relationships, context, and intent before producing a mask [8].

The concrete way this is done is via LISA [8], which introduced a single learned token, [SEG], as the interface between language reasoning and mask output. This is the foundation SegLLM builds on.

What is the [SEG] Token?
[SEG] is a single new token added to the LLM's vocabulary. Its job is not to carry meaning on its own; it simply signals to the rest of the system that a segmentation mask needs to be produced here, telling the downstream mask decoder when to act. What matters more than the token itself is its hidden state: the vector the LLM computes internally at the position of that token, taken from the model's last hidden layer. Because the LLM has just reasoned through the entire query and image together, this vector ends up containing exactly the semantic information the mask decoder needs, including what the task is, which object is being referred to, and how that object relates to others in the scene. Since this hidden state is what actually gets passed forward and compared against the image features, it is often referred to as the query vector.
Lai et al., CVPR 2024 [8]

Adding an LLM changes the three-part architecture: the encoders no longer feed the mask decoder directly. Instead, their outputs go into an LLM that reasons over both, and only the LLM's output (the [SEG] token's hidden state) is passed on to the mask decoder:

Figure 2: Reasoning segmentation architecture. The LLM sits between the encoders and the mask decoder, reasoning over both modalities before emitting the [SEG] token that drives mask prediction.

Note: in many modern approaches, the "LLM" here is actually a multimodal VLM (Vision-Language Model), which already has the components needed to handle both image and text internally. In that case, a separate text encoder is not needed, since the user's text query is fed straight into the model, which tokenizes and processes it itself alongside the visual input. This is exactly the case for LLaVA-based systems like LISA and SegLLM.
1.4 The Problem: Single-turn Limitation

The LLM in Figure 2 only ever sees each query once. Once a mask is generated, the model has no memory of it, and users cannot follow up with queries that reference previous segmentation outputs.

In Figure 2, information only flows in one direction: the image and text go into the LLM, the LLM emits a [SEG] token, and the mask decoder turns that into a mask. Once the mask decoder has produced its output, the pipeline is finished. There is no path for that mask, or any other trace of what just happened, to flow back into the LLM. This means that if a user asks a follow-up question that depends on the previous result, the LLM has no way to access what it just segmented, and it has no record that a previous round of segmentation even took place. Supporting multi-round reasoning would require exactly this feedback path, which Figure 2 does not have.

Round 1"Highlight the man with the white hoodie."
Round 2"Mask the bike behind the man from the previous prompt."

Without access to Round 1's mask, the model has no way to resolve "the man from the previous prompt".

You might think this is easy to fix. When we use a chatbot such as ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini, we can have a long, multi-turn conversation, and the model clearly remembers what was said earlier. Since reasoning segmentation is built on the same kind of LLM, it seems reasonable to expect that it should be able to do the same. Section 2 shows why it isn't.

02 Method

SegLLM adds the missing feedback path. After each round, the mask the decoder just produced is encoded and fed back into the LLM as part of the next round's input, so the LLM entering round 2 has direct access to what was segmented in round 1. This turns the one-way pipeline from Figure 2 into a loop.

Figure 3: Simplified architecture diagram. The Mask Encoder sits alongside the Image and Text Encoders and feeds the previous round's masks and information back into the LLM, closing the loop that Figure 2 was missing.

2.1 Model Architecture

SegLLM fills in the abstract architecture from Figure 2 with concrete models, and adds one new component for the feedback loop. On the input side, the image is processed by a pre-trained image encoder (CLIP-ViT-Large), which turns it into patch embeddings. These embeddings live in a different space than the LLM's text embeddings, so a small projection layer (an MLP) maps them into the LLM's own embedding space, letting image and text be processed as tokens in one shared sequence.

The text query does not go through a separate text encoder. The backbone LLM here is LLaVA-v1.5-7B, a multimodal VLM, so the text is tokenized and embedded using the LLM's own embedding layer, just like any other text input.

The new component is the Mask Encoder. Its job is to take the mask produced in a previous round and turn it into embeddings that go back into the LLM alongside the current image and text. To handle references across rounds, the LLM also uses a second special token alongside [SEG], called [REF]. Where [SEG] marks the object to be segmented now, [REF] marks an earlier object the current query is relating back to. Having two tokens lets the LLM distinguish "the new object I need to segment" from "the earlier object this query is referring to".

What is CLIP?
CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining) is a model developed by OpenAI that learns to connect images and text by training on hundreds of millions of image-caption pairs. Instead of learning to classify images into fixed categories, CLIP learns a shared embedding space where similar images and texts are placed close together. This makes it very good at understanding visual content through natural language, even for concepts it has never seen labelled before.
Radford et al., ICML 2021 [10]
What is LLaVA?
LLaVA (Large Language and Vision Assistant) is a multimodal model that connects a visual encoder with a large language model through a simple projection layer. It is fine-tuned on visual instruction data, teaching the model to answer questions and follow instructions about images. LLaVA serves as the backbone LLM in SegLLM, providing the language reasoning capability that drives the multi-round conversation.
Liu et al., NeurIPS 2023 [9]

The concrete backbone models for each component are:

Model Components

2.2 Mask Encoding Module

The Mask Encoder takes one previously produced mask, together with the original image, and outputs two compact embeddings the LLM can consume, rather than a whole new set of image patch tokens. Using single embeddings matters because re-encoding the full image for every previous mask would make the input sequence grow very quickly across rounds, whereas one or two extra tokens per mask does not.

The first of the two embeddings, the mask embedding, captures what the masked object looks like. To build it, every pixel outside the mask is set to black, and the image is cropped to the mask's bounding box, leaving an object-centric image that isolates just the segmented object. This cropped image is passed through a CLIP-ViT encoder to obtain a raw embedding, which is then projected through an MLP layer so its dimension matches the LLM's input embeddings.

Mask → black out + crop to bbox → CLIP-ViT encoder → MLP projection → Mask Embedding

Figure 4a: Mask embedding generation pipeline.

The second embedding, the bounding box embedding, captures where the object was located in the original image. A segmentation mask is just a set of foreground pixels, so its bounding box does not need to be predicted separately: it is computed directly from the mask, by taking the minimum and maximum x and y coordinates of the mask's foreground region. This gives the box in its plain coordinate form, (x1, y1, x2, y2), where (x1, y1) is the top-left corner and (x2, y2) is the bottom-right corner. These four numbers are then turned into a positional embedding of the same dimension as the LLM's input. No separate encoder network is needed for this step.

Mask → (x1, y1, x2, y2) → positional embedding → Box Embedding

Figure 4b: Bounding box embedding generation pipeline.

For every mask, these two embeddings, mask embedding first and then bounding box embedding, are fed into the LLM's input sequence one after another. Across multiple rounds, this gives the LLM a compact but complete record of everything segmented so far: what each object looked like, and where it was.

Design choice: both the mask embedding and the bounding box embedding are restricted to a single token, one vector each, rather than a full grid of image patch tokens per mask. This is deliberate: if every previous mask added a whole new set of patch tokens, the input sequence would grow very quickly as the conversation continues over multiple rounds. Since the full image has already been tokenized once for the current round, one compact token per mask is enough to tell the LLM what that object was and where it sat, without repeating information the model can already access.
2.3 Mask-Aware Decoding Module

An ordinary mask decoder, like the one in SAM or LISA, only ever sees the current query and the current image features; it has no access to what was segmented earlier in the conversation. SegLLM's mask decoder is made aware of that history directly, so it can take previous segmentation results into account when producing a new mask, rather than treating every round as a fresh, isolated request.

Its input is the hidden states of two special tokens the LLM emits, [REF] and [SEG]; its output is two separate masks, one that grounds a previously segmented object and one that is the actual answer to the current query.

Consider the query "segment the head of the person we identified earlier." Here, [REF] carries information about the person identified earlier, the reference object the query is relating to. [SEG] carries information about the actual target, the head. Without [REF], the decoder would have no way of knowing which person's head is meant.

To make use of both tokens, the decoder does not run once: it forms two separate queries, each handling one part of the task. The first query combines [REF] with a padding token, [PAD], and is matched only against the previously segmented object. This grounds [REF] to a specific region of the image, without asking the decoder to segment anything new.

[REF] hidden state + [PAD] → mask decoder → Reference Mask

Figure 5a: The first query, grounding [REF] to the previously segmented object.

The second query combines [REF] with [SEG], and is matched against the actual target: the object the current round should segment. Because [REF] is still present in this second query, the decoder can use the grounded reference location together with the new segmentation signal to resolve exactly what "the head" refers to.

[REF] hidden state + [SEG] hidden state → mask decoder → Target Mask

Figure 5b: The second query, resolving [SEG] in light of the grounded [REF].

Both queries run at inference time, not only during training. At each round the decoder produces both the reference mask (from [REF][PAD]) and the target mask (from [REF][SEG]); the target mask is what the user sees, while the reference mask acts as a check that [REF] is still grounded to the right object.

In the first round, or whenever a query does not depend on any earlier result, the LLM simply does not emit [REF], the same way it learns when to emit [SEG] in the first place. In that case the decoder falls back to a single [SEG]-only query, exactly like LISA's single-round setup. This is also why SegLLM matches or exceeds LISA on standard single-round benchmarks such as RefCOCO: when there is nothing to refer back to, it behaves just like a single-round model.

Design choice: both queries are trained with their own segmentation loss, so grounding the reference object is not left as a side effect of segmenting the target; it is directly supervised as its own task. This is what lets the decoder correctly resolve queries like "the head of the person we identified earlier", rather than only ever being trained to produce the final answer.

03 Training
3.1 Dataset

The training data consists of multi-round conversational segmentation queries built on top of several existing public datasets, including RefCOCO, Visual Genome, PACO-LVIS, LVIS, Pascal Panoptic Part, ADE20K, and COCO-Stuff, among others. Since none of these datasets originally come as conversations, they have to be turned into one: starting from a source dataset, the pipeline first selects a set of instances in an image, works out the relationships between them, and then fits those instances and relationships into conversational templates, which are refined with GPT-4 afterwards so the final questions read naturally.

source dataset → select instances → generate relationships → fit into conversation template → refined by GPT-4

Figure 6: The MRSeg dataset construction pipeline.

Which relationship type a conversation uses depends on which source dataset it is drawn from, since each dataset already provides a different kind of connection between objects. Hierarchical relationships, where a query asks about a sub-part of something already segmented, for example "the hand of" a person, come from PACO-LVIS and Pascal Panoptic Part, since these datasets already label parts alongside their parent objects. Positional relationships, where a query refers to an object by its location relative to a previous one, for example "to the left of", are drawn mainly from RefCOCO(+/g) and LVIS. Interactional relationships, where a query refers to what an object is doing, for example "looking at", come from datasets such as Visual Genome, which annotate actions and interactions between objects directly.

Not every source dataset can support long conversations either. Some, like Visual Genome or COCO-Stuff, mostly provide single-round queries, while others, like RefCOCO or PACO-LVIS, are rich enough in related instances to support conversations of well over ten rounds. This range is intentional: it lets SegLLM see both short, simple queries and long, relationship-heavy ones during training.

Figure 7: Dataset overview showing multi-round conversational segmentation examples across different object relationship types.

What is RefCOCO?
RefCOCO is a referring expression dataset built on top of MS-COCO images. It was collected using a two-player game called ReferItGame: one player is shown an object highlighted in an image and writes a short description of it, while a second player has to pick out the correct object from that description alone, with no other cues. This produces natural, human-written referring expressions rather than templated ones. The dataset covers around 142,000 expressions for roughly 50,000 objects across about 20,000 images, split so that one test set is dominated by images containing people and the other by everything else. Since players were free to describe objects however they liked, including by location, RefCOCO expressions frequently use spatial words such as "left" and "right".
Yu et al., ECCV 2016 [19]
3.2 Training

Only the LLM and the projection layer are trained. Every other component (the image encoder, the mask encoder, and the mask decoder) uses a pre-trained model with its parameters kept completely frozen. The training below is therefore entirely about teaching the LLM to reason correctly and to emit the right tokens at the right time, not about learning to see or to draw masks from scratch.

SegLLM's training extends LISA's single-token setup, so we start there:

Why exactly "It is [SEG]"?
USER: <IMAGE> Can you segment the {class name} in this image?
ASSISTANT: It is [SEG].

LISA trains the LLM on templated question-answer pairs like this one, where [SEG] is simply part of the target sentence. The paper does not explain why it settled on this specific wording, but we suspect it comes down to engineering convenience. A short, highly regular template like this guarantees that [SEG] always appears in a predictable position in the output, which makes both the training data and the extraction of its hidden state much simpler to implement.

There is also a semantic argument for it: if the LLM outputs "it is" followed by a single token, that token is, grammatically, whatever "it" refers to, which is exactly the content we want the query vector to carry.

Allowing free-form responses, or a more diverse set of templates, would likely require extra processing to locate [SEG] and to keep its meaning well defined, so we read this as a deliberate simplification, a trick, rather than a principled design choice. This reading lines up with how the field later reacted to it: LISA++ [11] explicitly criticises this template as too rigid and unnatural, arguing it cannot adapt to the diversity of responses needed in real applications.

Because [SEG] is treated as an ordinary word in the answer, learning to place it correctly requires nothing beyond the standard autoregressive cross-entropy loss already used to train any LLM: the model is simply taught to predict the correct next word at every position, and one of those words happens to be [SEG]. No separate classifier or rule decides when to segment; the language modelling objective already covers it.

SegLLM applies this same mechanism to both [REF] and [SEG]. Compared with single-round models like LISA, which need exactly one mask per training example, SegLLM has to handle multiple masks within a single multi-round conversation, which is far more expensive to train on. To keep this computationally feasible, the authors replace SAM's large ViT-H mask decoder with a smaller HIPIE-R50 decoder. This mask decoder, along with the CLIP image encoder, is kept frozen throughout training; only the LLM and the projection layer connecting it to the visual encoder are fine-tuned.

At training time, the ground-truth mask and box embeddings are inserted directly after each [SEG] token, rather than the model's own predictions. This is called teacher forcing, and it is standard practice for training sequence models: it stops small early mistakes from throwing off everything that follows, so the model always learns from a clean, correct history. At inference time, no ground truth is available, so the model instead feeds its own previous outputs back in.

The training follows a two-objective setup. The language modelling loss is the same autoregressive cross-entropy loss just described for [SEG], extended to also cover [REF]. The segmentation loss puts SegLLM's two-query design into a concrete formula:

Lmask = Lseg( [REF],[PAD] , Mref ) + Lseg( [REF],[SEG] , Mtgt )
where  Lseg = Lce + λ·LDICE   (λ = 1)

First, notice it is a sum of two separate terms, one for the reference mask Mref and one for the target mask Mtgt. This is the two-query design written out as maths: grounding the reference object is never left as a side effect of getting the target right, it has its own loss term and is supervised directly.

Second, each of those two terms is itself a combination of cross-entropy (Lce) and DICE loss (LDICE), a standard pairing in segmentation, since the two catch different kinds of mistakes:

Cross-Entropy
Measures how far a predicted probability is from the correct answer, one of the most common loss functions in machine learning. In segmentation, this is applied per pixel: each pixel is a small classification problem, "is this pixel part of the object or not". It is stable, but most pixels in any image are background, so a model could get a deceptively low loss just by predicting "background" everywhere. This is also the same loss used for language modelling, just applied to predicting the next word instead of a pixel's class, which is why the same term appears both in Lseg here and in the language modelling loss described above.
DICE Loss
Built from the DICE coefficient, a measure of overlap between two regions, similar to IoU: highest when the predicted and ground-truth masks line up perfectly, lowest when they do not overlap at all. Because it judges the two regions as a whole rather than pixel by pixel, it does not care how many background pixels surround the object, only how well the foreground regions match. This makes it far less sensitive to class imbalance than cross-entropy.

Combined, they give a stable pixel-level gradient (cross-entropy) plus a shape-focused, imbalance-resistant signal (DICE).

04 Results

SegLLM is evaluated on three tasks, all benchmarked against LISA. The third, Multi-round Segmentation (MRSeg), covers query types of increasing complexity, so it is reported as cIoU per round rather than a single number:

Task Metric Description
Referring Expression Segmentation (RES) cIoU Cumulative Intersection over Union, measuring the ratio of intersection to union of predicted and ground truth masks accumulated across all test samples.
Referring Expression Comprehension (REC) Acc@0.5 Percentage of predictions where the predicted bounding box has an IoU of at least 0.5 with the ground truth box.
Multi-round Segmentation (MRSeg) cIoU per round cIoU reported per conversational round, evaluating how well the model maintains segmentation accuracy as the number of rounds and relational complexity increases.

SegLLM consistently outperforms both LISA and GLaMM, another multi-round baseline, across all rounds on MRSeg, with the gap widening in later rounds where cross-round reasoning matters most. On single-round RefCOCO, the improvement shows up in absolute numbers too: SegLLM reaches 81.5 cIoU on the testA split, against LISA's 76.5.

05 Our Opinion

SegLLM beats LISA by 18–30% on MRSeg and still gains 5 points on single-round RefCOCO. The multi-round follow-up problem it targets, asking about an object relative to one segmented earlier, is one we had not seen addressed before, and the fix is architecturally clean.

Five concerns stood out to us. Where we had a concrete suggestion, we've included one.

01 Error propagation

If the model makes a wrong segmentation in round one, that error may carry into all subsequent rounds, since each round builds directly on top of previous outputs. The Mask Encoding Module encodes the wrong mask's bounding box straight into the LLM's input stream, with no signal to the model that this coordinate is uncertain. The MRSeg results already hint at this: cIoU declines as rounds increase, which may partly reflect accumulated error rather than just increased task complexity. It would be valuable to know how the model performs when intentionally given a wrong first-round mask.

Possible direction

Part of the problem is that SegLLM never explains itself. The LLM goes straight from the query to the [SEG] token, with no explicit reasoning step in between, so when an error does occur, there is no trace showing which object the model thought it was looking at, or why. Many other reasoning tasks now follow a different pipeline: the model first states its reasoning for a judgment, then produces the judgment itself, sometimes with a confidence score attached. Applying this here would mean having the LLM state, in each round, which object it believes a query refers to and why, before emitting [SEG]. This alone would not stop errors from happening, but it would make them visible, rather than hidden inside a single forward pass into a mask.

Once that reasoning trace exists, a strategy similar to Step-DPO [15] becomes possible. Step-DPO improves long-chain mathematical reasoning by locating the specific step where a reasoning chain goes wrong, using a strong LLM to identify the error and generate a corrected version of that step, and then training the model on this corrected step directly. Applied to SegLLM, each conversational round could be treated the way Step-DPO treats a reasoning step: after generating a batch of multi-round conversations, a strong LLM could review the reasoning trace at each round, flag the round where the reference to a previous object first goes wrong, and produce a corrected version. Training on this kind of round-level correction data could directly target the accumulation problem described above, rather than only supervising the final mask in each round.

02 Order sensitivity

The model appears sensitive to conversation ordering, since the [REF] token mechanism is built up sequentially with no global attention over all historical rounds. For example, asking "segment the person" first and then "segment the bag she is holding" may give a different result than asking about the bag before establishing the person as a reference. NLP evaluation routinely uses reversed options and paraphrased queries as robustness tests; the same approach fits here. Reordering conversation histories would show whether the model actually understands the relationships, or is just pattern-matching prompt order.

Possible direction

This looks like the same underlying problem as positional-keyword sensitivity: the model does not seem to fully understand the query, or in this case the conversation as a whole, independently of the specific order it was presented in. The two failures differ in what is being varied, though, so the fix should differ too. Paraphrasing changes the wording of a single query, while here it is the sequence of rounds that changes; simply rewording queries would not teach the model anything about ordering.

A more direct option is to build a dataset of reordered conversations: taking existing multi-round examples and generating several permutations of the round order, then training the model to give the same answer regardless of which permutation it sees. This kind of order-permutation augmentation has been shown to help in other settings. Xiang et al. [17] address order sensitivity of in-context examples in causal language models directly, using multiple permutations together with a consistency loss so the model's output no longer depends on which position an example appears in. Zhang et al. [18] take a similar approach for positional bias more broadly, constructing augmented training data in which every candidate appears at every possible position. Neither paper works on multi-round segmentation specifically, but both support generating a permuted-order dataset as a reasonable way to reduce SegLLM's sensitivity to conversation ordering.

Generating every possible permutation of a conversation's rounds gets expensive quickly as the number of rounds grows, since the number of orderings grows factorially. A simpler starting point would be to just reverse the original round order for each conversation. This is a single, cheap augmentation per example rather than a full permutation set, and it still gives the model a meaningfully different ordering to learn from, which should be enough to test whether even this minimal amount of order variation improves consistency before committing to the more expensive full-permutation approach.

03 Scalability over long conversations

As the number of rounds increases, the mask embeddings and bounding box embeddings fed back into the LLM grow linearly. The paper only evaluates up to 8 rounds on the MRSeg benchmark. In longer real-world conversations, such as a radiologist annotating dozens of structures in a single session, the context window may become a limiting factor. It is unclear how the model degrades when the number of historical mask embeddings exceeds what the LLM can effectively attend to.

Possible direction

With two tokens per mask, a nine-round conversation adds 18 tokens to the input, on top of the image patch tokens and text. At the scale of a radiology session with dozens of structures, this alone would push against LLaVA-7B's context window. Production chat tools like Claude even ship a manual /compact command to summarise and shrink conversation history when it grows too long, evidence that unbounded context growth is already a practical problem in deployed systems.

One option is to make the mask encoding itself recurrent, rather than independent per round. Right now, each round's mask and box embeddings are computed only from that round's mask, then simply appended to the growing input. An alternative would be to compute each new embedding from both the current mask and a running summary of everything segmented so far, similar to how a Transformer's hidden states carry information forward from earlier tokens, or how an RNN's hidden state is updated at each step. This is close to what Video Detective [16] does for long video understanding: instead of storing a token per segment indefinitely, it recurrently aggregates and updates a small set of memory tokens as new segments arrive, so history is carried forward in a compressed form rather than accumulated in full. Similar designs already exist in the video-understanding literature, where each frame is compressed down to a small, fixed number of tokens tied together by a memory mechanism, rather than letting the token count grow with every frame. Applying the same idea to SegLLM's mask history could keep the input size closer to constant, or at least growing more slowly, even as the number of rounds goes well beyond what the paper currently tests.

04 No correction mechanism

If a user realises that a mask from an earlier round was wrong, there is no way to correct it within the framework. The incorrect mask has already been encoded into the conversation history and will continue to influence all future rounds. A practical deployment of SegLLM would need some form of undo or correction interface, but the paper does not address this. This matters especially in medical settings, where interactive segmentation already cuts annotation workload by 3–4× compared to manual labelling [2], but errors in that workflow can have serious downstream consequences.

05 Sensitivity to positional keywords

The authors' own qualitative evaluation reports a second, related weakness: the model is highly sensitive to positional words like "left" and "right" within a single query. In one test, they construct a query that includes the word "left" while actually asking for the person on the right, and another that includes the word "right" while asking for the person on the left, similar to a phrasing such as "not the person on the right, but the one on the left". In both cases the model segments whichever side is named by the positional word, regardless of how that word is actually used in the sentence. This is a more specific failure than plain keyword sensitivity: the query contains two positional words in the same sentence, one of them negated or contrasted against the other, and the model does not appear to process that structure at all. The authors' own reading is that the model may be paying more attention to isolated positional words than to the meaning of the full expression.

Possible direction

This points, at least in part, to the model not fully understanding how positional information and semantic content are meant to combine within a query. One option is targeted paraphrase augmentation: generating two or three paraphrased versions of existing queries, for example with a strong model such as GPT-5, focused specifically on the positional keyword rather than the whole sentence. Given the trap construction described above, this should go beyond simple synonym swaps such as rewording "on the right" as "to the right of". It should also include queries with two positional words in the same sentence, one negated or contrasted against the other, so the model is trained to resolve the full structure rather than reacting to whichever keyword appears first.

The paper's own "diverse query templates" ablation, which showed a +9.6% improvement on RefCOCO, tested variation in the overall phrasing and structure of queries, not variation targeted at positional keywords specifically. This is evidence that broad template diversity helps generalisation in general, but it does not show whether positional-keyword paraphrasing would fix this particular failure. Testing that would need a dedicated follow-up experiment, holding the rest of the query fixed while varying only how the positional relation is expressed, and evaluating on cases built to catch keyword-based shortcuts like the one above.

This general direction is supported by work outside this paper. SPARTA [12] studies the same kind of vulnerability directly in reasoning segmentation: grammatically correct paraphrases that preserve a query's meaning can still cause segmentation performance to collapse, suggesting that models in this space tend to rely on surface phrasing rather than robustly understanding the query. SpaRE [13] shows that training on more diverse spatial descriptions leads vision-language models toward a more robust understanding of spatial concepts, rather than models that only work for the specific phrasings seen in training. Hoehing et al. [14] tackle left-right confusion directly, showing that carefully constructed contrastive sentence pairs can improve left-right understanding in CLIP-style models. None of these three papers test positional-keyword paraphrasing within reasoning segmentation specifically, but together they support the direction proposed here. Hoehing et al. was published in late 2023, a year before SegLLM. The fix for left-right confusion was already in the literature; we do not know why SegLLM did not use it. One plausible reason: training on this scale, across multiple rounds and multiple masks per conversation, already stretched the available compute, leaving little room for an additional round of targeted augmentation.

Together with the ordering issue, this leaves an unresolved question: is the model taking shortcuts from training data statistics, or does it fail to compose the query at all? The paper's evaluation does not separate these two. Either way, the model currently cannot meet the medical imaging segmentation requirements we described at the beginning of this post.

Takeaways
Feedback loop Closes the gap between mask decoder and LLM.
[SEG] and [REF] Two tokens, two queries: ground the reference, then segment the target.
One token per mask Avoids re-encoding the image, but still grows every round.
Strong results Beats LISA on both multi-round and single-round benchmarks.
Still brittle Sensitive to conversation order and positional keywords.
Not medical-grade yet Reliability gaps remain too large for high-stakes use.
